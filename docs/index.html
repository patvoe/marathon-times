Marathon Times: Elite Runners Continuously Getting Faster

World records are tumbling on the marathon course. Are the recent records just outliers, or is there a trend towards ever faster times? A data analysis shows how the top times of the elite have changed over the last 20 years.

Ethiopia's Tigist Assefa crosses the finish line to win the women's division of the Berlin Marathon in world record time in Berlin.
Foto: Markus Schreiber (Keystone)

By Patrick Vögeli

One world record after another is being set over the legendary 42.195-kilometer distance. The Kenyan Kelvin Kiptum, who tragically died in a car accident in February at just 24 years old, broke his compatriot Eliud Kipchoge’s old record by 34 seconds a month later in Chicago. The 23-year-old crossed the finish line in two hours and 35 seconds. The magical two-hour barrier seems just a heartbeat away.

And the Ethiopian Tigist Assefa, who seemingly came out of nowhere, broke the women’s world record by over two minutes in Berlin in the fall of 2023 with a time 2:11:53.

The recent records are not outliers, as shown by the data from the World Athletics (WA). The times of the 100 fastest men in the marathon have significantly improved over the past 20 years.

While the fastest men needed an average of 2:09:43 to cover the 42.195 kilometers in 2004, today it takes only 2:05:27. That's 4 minutes and 16 seconds less.

The difference is even greater among women. While the 100 fastest women needed an average 2:28:41 in 2004, today it takes only 2:21:14. That's 7 minutes and 27 seconds less.

For the World Athletics (WA) federation, the numerous records are not an unusual phenomenon. «Throughout the history of our sport, world records have always been set with the best available technology, and that is no different today,» WA explained in response to a dpa inquiry.

When they say technology, they mainly mean footwear. In 2017, Nike released the innovative running shoe called Vaporfly 4%, the first marathon-specific shoes with a carbon-fiber plate inside super-responsive foam. An analysis by the New York Times, using data from the fitness app Strava, found that runners wearing either the Vaporfly 4% or Vaporfly Next% ran 4 to 5 percent faster than those in average shoes. These runners also ran 2 to 3 percent faster than those in the next-fastest shoe. By the end of 2019, about 41 percent of sub-three-hour marathons were run in these Nike shoes.

Apart from technology, according to experts, improved training techniques, more competitions, refined tracks, or a more sophisticated diet are additional importent reasons for record improvements in the marathon.

However, the data from the fastest runners, as shown by World Athletics, reveals something else. The participation of athletes of African origin, particularly from East Africa, has significantly transformed marathon running. Today, runners from countries like Kenya and Ethiopia dominate the sport. Of the 200 fastest times (men and women), almost 70 percent (137) were set by athletes from Ethiopia or Kenya. Especially Ethiopia is a rising star in the marathon scene in the last two decade.
